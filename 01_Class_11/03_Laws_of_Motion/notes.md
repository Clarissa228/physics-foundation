# Topic 3: Laws of Motion

## Overview
Newton's three laws of motion form the foundation of classical mechanics. This topic explains how forces change motion and how objects interact through forces and acceleration.

## Key Concepts

### 3.1 Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)
An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity unless acted upon by an external force.

**Inertia:** The property of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia.

**Inertial Reference Frame:** A reference frame in which Newton's first law is valid (no acceleration of the frame itself).

### 3.2 Newton's Second Law (Law of Force and Acceleration)
The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$$

Or in component form:
$$F_x = ma_x, \quad F_y = ma_y, \quad F_z = ma_z$$

**Key implications:**
- Greater force produces greater acceleration
- Greater mass produces smaller acceleration (for same force)
- Direction of acceleration is same as direction of net force
- Force has units: Newton (N) = kg·m/s²

### 3.3 Newton's Third Law (Law of Action and Reaction)
If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

$$\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$$

**Important distinction:** These forces act on different objects, so they don't cancel out. They are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

### 3.4 Types of Forces

**Contact Forces:**
- Normal force: $\vec{N}$ - perpendicular to surface, prevents objects from occupying same space
- Friction: $\vec{f}$ - opposes relative motion between surfaces
- Tension: $\vec{T}$ - pulls objects together along a rope or string
- Applied force: $\vec{F}_{app}$ - force directly applied to object

**Non-contact (Field) Forces:**
- Gravitational force: $\vec{F}_g = m\vec{g}$ (weight = $mg$, where $g \approx 9.81$ m/s²)
- Electromagnetic forces
- Nuclear forces

### 3.5 Friction

**Static Friction** - prevents object from starting to move:
$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$

where $\mu_s$ is coefficient of static friction.

Maximum static friction: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$

**Kinetic (Sliding) Friction** - opposes motion:
$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

where $\mu_k$ is coefficient of kinetic friction (typically $\mu_k < \mu_s$).

**Rolling Friction** - resistance to rolling motion:
$$f_{roll} = \mu_{roll} N$$

Much smaller than kinetic friction for well-designed wheels.

### 3.6 Applications: Inclined Plane
For object on incline at angle $\theta$:
- Component parallel to plane: $mg\sin\theta$
- Component perpendicular to plane: $mg\cos\theta$

**Critical angle for sliding:** $\tan\theta = \mu_s$ (angle at which object just begins to slide)

### 3.7 Circular Motion and Centripetal Force
For object moving in circle at constant speed:

**Centripetal acceleration:** $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2$ (directed toward center)

**Centripetal force:** $F_c = ma_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2$

This force is provided by tension, friction, gravity, or normal force depending on the situation.

### 3.8 Pseudo-Forces in Non-Inertial Frames

In an accelerating reference frame, fictitious forces appear:

**Centrifugal force** (in rotating frame): $\vec{F}_{cf} = m\omega^2 r$ (radially outward)

**Pseudo-force in accelerating frame:** $\vec{F}_{pseudo} = -m\vec{a}_{frame}$

## Key Equations (Summary)

$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$$ (Newton's 2nd Law)

$$f_s \leq \mu_s N$$ (Static Friction)

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$ (Kinetic Friction)

$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2$$ (Centripetal Force)

$$\tau = I\alpha$$ (Rotational analog of F=ma)

$$\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$$ (Newton's 3rd Law)

## Historical Context

**Isaac Newton (1642-1726)** published his three laws in "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica" (Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy) in 1687. These laws unified terrestrial mechanics with celestial mechanics and became the foundation for all classical physics.
